## Prompt 1 — The Problem

University students consistently struggle with study inefficiency; not because they lack effort, but because they lack data. Most students have no systematic way of knowing which locations or times of day produce their best focus. Manually tracking this is tedious, easy to forget, and prone to miscalculation. Without this insight, students repeat unproductive habits unknowingly, leading to wasted time and eventual burnout. AI is needed because identifying meaningful patterns across sessions, locations, times, and break frequency requires analysis that goes far beyond what a student could reasonably do themselves.

---

## Prompt 2 — Who Are the Users and How Does RIACT Affect Their Lives

RIACT's primary users are undergraduate and graduate students who struggle to understand their own study habits. As undergraduate students ourselves, we designed RIACT from lived experience; knowing firsthand how difficult it is to identify when and where you truly focus best. Beyond students, RIACT applies to anyone tracking knowledge and productivity. The website directly improves users' lives by replacing guesswork with data; showing them exactly which locations and times produce their best work, so they can make smarter decisions about how they spend their limited study time.

---

## Prompt 3 — AI Approach

RIACT uses AI across three core functions. First, pattern recognition: the AI analyzes net study time versus break frequency across locations and times of day to identify where and when a user is truly most focused. Second, burnout detection: the AI monitors signals including shorter sessions, more frequent breaks, late-night cramming, and declining goal completion to warn users before burnout hits. Third, personalized recommendations: the AI translates patterns into specific, actionable guidance such as 'your peak focus window is Tuesday mornings at SFU Bennett Library.' AI is the right tool here because these patterns emerge across dozens of data points over time; this is far beyond what any student could reasonably detect manually or through a spreadsheet.

---

## Prompt 4 — Ethical Considerations

The primary ethical risk of RIACT is that burnout detection could be mistaken for medical diagnosis. A student receiving a burnout warning may treat it as clinical fact rather than a data-driven signal. To mitigate this, every burnout warning in RIACT is clearly labeled as a recommendation only, not a medical assessment. The AI identifies patterns that suggest risk, but explicitly cannot and does not diagnose burnout. All warnings include a reminder to consult a medical or mental health professional if needed, and links to university mental health resources are surfaced alongside every warning. The human, not the AI, decides what action to take.
